The World Today...

Today, the presence of climate change is undeniable — its effects are visible across every continent and ocean.

Global atmospheric carbon dioxide levels have surpassed 420 ppm, the highest in at least 800,000 years — up from just 310 ppm eighty years ago. Average global temperatures are now about 1.2°C warmer than in pre-industrial times, driving more frequent and severe heatwaves, storms, and floods.

Sea levels have risen by over 10 cm since the early 1990s, with the current rate—about 3.9 mm per year—almost triple that of the early 20th century. Polar ice is shrinking rapidly: the Arctic summer sea ice minimum has fallen by nearly 40% compared to the mid-20th century.

Agriculture now shows clear vulnerability — climate change has reduced global crop productivity by an estimated 21% since the 1960s. Regions like Africa and Latin America face worsening droughts and lower yields, threatening food security worldwide.

Human health and safety are increasingly at risk: around 3.3 to 3.6 billion people live in areas highly vulnerable to climate impacts, while disasters now affect over 124 million people annually. Economic losses average more than US$200 billion each year.

The contrast between the relatively stable climate of the 1940s and today’s disrupted systems shows the urgency for immediate, coordinated action. While “doomsday” narratives can sound exaggerated, the evidence is clear — we are at a turning point. The next decade will determine whether humanity acts responsibly or faces irreversible consequences.
